Death and Out-Migration: How Generations Get Smaller

Since out-migration from the US is low, death is the primary way that US generations shrink over time. The Social Security Administration releases statistics on how many Americans die each year and the age at which they died. If we assume that future people at each age will have the same chances of dying before the next birthday, we can begin to project how quickly each generation's numbers will deplete over time. This calculation is the basis for life expectancy statistics.

Disasters, technological innovations, and other big societal events will change life expectancies over time. So, while these figures are probably very accurate in the near term, they become less accurate as the projections stretch further into the future.


Birth and In-Migration: How Generations Get Bigger

Birth and in-migration are the primary ways that US generations grow over time. Boomer cohorts were especially large (the eponymous "baby boom") because the conditions for raising a family were more conducive in the post-war West than they had been during WWII or the Great Depression. Subsequent cohorts have been smaller because, world-wide, people in developed countries have been having fewer children (the "demographic transition"). The Census Bureau releases detailed population projections based on these steady trends in birth rates.

In-migration rates depend on US immigration policy — a politically contentious issue. If policy keeps rates low, then cohorts attain maximum size at the end of their birth year, and deaths steady diminishes their numbers in subsequent years. Age and Cohort Effects: How Generations Lean Politically

Combining our projections for cohort births, deaths and migration, we can make rough projections about the size of each cohort over time. To the extent that numerical superiority translates into power, these projections tell us how much power each cohort may have over the political process in a given year. But what policies and politicians will each cohort support with this power?

On average, older Americans lean conservative and younger Americans lean liberal. However, that can either be attributed to age or cohort. If conservatism is attributable to age, then today's young liberals will become tomorrow's old conservatives because the experience of being older inclines one to favor conservatism. If attributable to cohort, today's young liberals become tomorrow's old liberals because getting older does not change their ideology.
